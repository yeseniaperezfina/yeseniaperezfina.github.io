Case study 04 · Digital interpretation

Do not just point. Interpret.

For Webb’s fourth anniversary, the first public STORIMap invited audiences inside Centaurus A. I helped turn a spectacular image into a navigable story—and then led the evaluation designed to improve the format’s future.

01

The problem

A beautiful image can still leave people outside.

Curious public users arrive image-first, with limited context and only seconds to decide whether to explore. The interactive had to orient them quickly, guide attention, explain why Webb’s view matters, and reward every click with understanding.

Centaurus A is visually rich but conceptually dense: a past galactic collision, warped dust, star formation, ancient stars, and an active center all compete for attention. The risk was an impressive interface that became a collection of labels—or a story that collapsed into “there is a black hole here.”

The interpretive rule

Every interaction should deepen the view.

01

Orient

What am I seeing, why does it matter, and what can I do here?

02

Guide

Use visual anchors and comparisons to direct attention—not merely decorate the image.

03

Interpret

Connect visible evidence to the galaxy’s history and Webb’s infrared capabilities.

04

Build

Let each stop add to a coherent mental model rather than repeat isolated facts.

02

My contribution

I gave the exploration a public-learning architecture.

I synthesized audience needs, science priorities, and interface constraints into the recommendation the team could build: one connected guided tour, supported by exploratory points of interest.

A

Audience brief

Defined likely user modes, product jobs, misconceptions, interaction risks, and the essential takeaway the experience needed to produce.

B

Story structure

Clarified the distinction between a narrative tour and independent discoveries, resolving overlap and protecting a strong story arc.

C

Interpretive review

Reviewed final text for audience clarity and helped connect Webb’s infrared view to merger history, dust, stars, and activity.

D

Implementation learning

Turned the pilot’s production experience into recommendations for kickoff, asset selection, roles, review, accessibility, and future planning.

Created collaboratively with writers, scientists, designers, developers, mission reviewers, and NASA partners. My contribution centered audience strategy, content structure, implementation learning, and evaluation leadership.

03

From product to system

The pilot became a framework for what comes next.

The implementation debrief surfaced a larger truth: STORIMap is not simply a visual extension of a press release. It is an audience-navigation tool that needs its own planning process.

Choose images intentionally

Assess resolution, visual richness, comparison assets, and whether the telescope reveals something the interaction can make legible.

Structure before drafting

Define the tour arc, POIs, audience questions, takeaways, and visual treatments before writing individual stops.

Assign decisions early

Clarify ownership across science, content, design, accessibility, development, and review at kickoff.

Design accessibility in

Integrate alternative descriptions, keyboard and screen-reader needs, and interaction behavior before the final build.

My role expanded

From shaping one map to improving the model.

I became the primary coordination lead for the external evaluation: framing the learning questions, aligning internal stakeholders, and distinguishing two related evidence streams.

Evaluation asks
Did the experience support comprehension, engagement, mission meaning, and interpretive value?
User testing asks
Could people notice, navigate, zoom, enter tours, and understand the interface?
The strategic question
What should be improved, refined, or carried forward into future STORIMaps—including Roman?

What this work proves

An interactive is not successful because people can click it. It succeeds when clicking changes what they can see.

STORIMap brings together the work I do best: translating emerging science, designing interpretive products, coordinating cross-functional decisions, and building the evidence systems that let an institution learn from what it makes.
