When does learning actually count?

My research asks what institutions owe people after learning has already happened—and what must change when recognition, belonging, or trust depends on navigating systems that were never designed to carry everyone equally.

The flagship inquiry

Recognition makes learning visible.
Application makes learning move.

Apprenticeship-to-degree pathways promise access by combining paid work, structured learning, and degree progress. Yet a learner can complete the work, demonstrate mastery, earn a credential—and still discover that the learning does not satisfy anything consequential.

My independent research argues that the missing mechanism is pre-committed application: defined evidence of validated learning satisfies a named degree, credential, or licensure requirement under approved conditions.

Scale cannot depend on heroics. Scale needs rules.

Contrasting governance cases

Depth without portability.
Portability without obligation.

Reach University

Embedded recognition

Reach redesigns the degree around workplace learning. Assessment and application are built into the model, so work counts inside the institution.

What it proves

Institutions can move judgment upstream and redistribute assessment authority.

What remains

Recognition that binds internally does not automatically travel to another institution.

RACC

Networked recognition

The Registered Apprenticeship–College Consortium helps apprenticeship learning travel through shared translation and credit-recognition structures.

What it proves

Systems can widen the legibility and portability of validated learning.

What remains

Recognized credit may still become elective credit—or return to case-by-case discretion.

The integrative view

Storytelling + systems.

The degree helped me connect the work that once looked separate: informal learning, science communication, postsecondary pathways, institutional governance, and leadership.

The throughline is not a sector. It is a way of seeing—follow the learner, locate the rule, study who holds authority, and design conditions in which people can move with dignity.

What I carry into practice

Five commitments sharpened at Harvard.

Move judgment upstream.

Rigor is strengthened when standards, evidence, authority, and application are decided clearly before an individual must petition.

Design beyond navigation.

Equity cannot mean teaching people to survive broken processes more skillfully. Systems should reduce the need for heroic navigation.

Treat belonging as infrastructure.

Belonging frees cognitive bandwidth; credible signals of mattering stabilize identity and make authorship possible.

Ask what rule the number creates.

Metrics and agreements allocate risk. A system becomes legible when we identify who is protected and who becomes the flexible variable.

Let reflection change the design.

Reflection is not retreat from action. It is disciplined interpretation that protects complexity from being flattened by urgency.

From scholarship to public voice

“Learning is not just about mastery, but about what we are willing to hold open for one another.”

At HGSE Convocation, I spoke about the hope I found in people refusing smallness, easy certainty, and the habit of treating people like problems to sort. “What Held” imagined a different future for education—one where attention is a form of care, making room is part of the work, and people with full lives make space for one another.
