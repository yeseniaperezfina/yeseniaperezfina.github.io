Leadership philosophy

I lead with a bias toward clarity, coherence, and care. My first move in any complex environment is to make the landscape legible: Who is here, what matters to them, what the system is asking for, and where the friction lives.

From there, I design structures and stories that people can actually move through: portfolios that align scattered efforts, convenings that surface what’s unsaid, and narratives that connect mission to daily decision-making.

I’m most alive in work that sits at the intersection of strategy and humanity—where rigorous thinking, creative insight, and relational leadership are all required for the system to hold.

Practice

How I move through complexity: the principles that ground my decisions and design.

See the whole system

I start by mapping the landscape—people, histories, incentives, constraints—so that decisions are made with an honest view of the system, not just the nearest fire.

Make complexity legible

I translate research, governance, and technical detail into stories and structures that people can actually act on—without flattening what matters.

Prototype with care

I treat pilots as real experiences for real people, not disposable experiments—designing iteratively while honoring the time, trust, and labor involved.

Design for survivability

I ask not just “Is this good?” but “Can people survive this?”—looking at workload, emotional load, and the long arc of implementation.

Build with, not for

I bring stakeholders in as co-designers and sensemakers, especially those closest to the impact of the work.

Honor context & place

I pay attention to place, history, and culture—because strategies that ignore context rarely hold.
