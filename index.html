Practice

My work tends to take five forms

I do not think in terms of isolated tasks. I think in systems: what the work is trying to do, who it needs to serve, and what has to be built around it for it to last.

Learning systems

I design structures that help complex work hold across contexts.

My strongest work sits at the level of architecture: building the supports, rhythms, and pathways that allow knowledge to move across institutions and audiences without losing rigor or usability.

  • Learning design strategy across distributed settings
  • Scalable models for educators, partners, and public audiences
  • Programs built for both coherence and local adaptation

Systems

A few places this work has lived

System 01 · NASA’s Universe of Learning

A national learning network built for local use

I direct a distributed ecosystem of museums, libraries, and community institutions, shaping the supports that allow NASA science to be adapted locally without losing coherence. The work is not just reach. It is usability, trust, and whether partners have what they need to carry the work forward.

  • Partner enablement, professional learning, and micro-grant design
  • Support models that strengthen sustainability and implementation
  • Evaluation-informed improvement across network cycles
System 02 · Webb milestone engagement

Turning scientific visibility into public learning

During major Webb moments, I helped build the infrastructure that turned scientific visibility into something educators, institutions, and communities could actually use in real time.

System 03 · Museum learning

A foundation in bilingual and intergenerational design

My museum work taught me what still matters most: pace, context, invitation, language, and the design choices that shape whether people feel welcomed into knowledge or kept at its edge.

System 04 · Higher education and reflection

Leadership shaped by belonging and judgment

My Harvard work has sharpened the leadership logic already present in practice: who gets to belong, who gets to decide what counts, and what kind of leadership protects rigor without losing humanity.

Leadership

What is often called outreach is, in practice, systems leadership.

Much of the work I do is quiet. It happens before the public-facing moment, inside the structures that make that moment possible.

What I am usually doing

Translating between expert and educator, aligning teams, protecting usability alongside rigor, and making sure the people carrying the work outward have what they need.

What I try to protect

Belonging as structure, not sentiment. Expectations that are legible. Reflection as method. Scale that remains human.

What teams rely on me for

Calm judgment under complexity. Strong synthesis. Pattern recognition. The ability to hold architecture and atmosphere in the same frame.

Why I build in code too

Interface, hierarchy, and interaction are forms of leadership. Working in the medium sharpens how I think about clarity, usability, and the decisions that shape audience experience.
